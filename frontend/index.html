Introduction

Background information

This study investigates the use and development of Large Language Model(LLM)'s that have been developed for the purpose of influencing and supporting grading systems at the college/university level. If you've ever had your phone auto complete a message when texting, that was basically what an LLM does. LLMs are really powerful pattern recognition programs that use learned patterns to predict the “correct” response. An LLM will try to “complete” the pattern that they see. The primary concern of this case study consists of identifying any ethical issues that may ensue from the deployment of such systems. This involves understanding how LLM's may affect different people in a discriminatory manner, a random manner, or in an unexpected way. The effect LLM's have against the stakeholders of this case will then be evaluated through different ethical frameworks in order to give the audience not a definite answer, but ideas on how standard ethical frameworks might approach any issues provided from the installment of LLM assisted graders.

Stakeholders

What is a stakeholder?

A stakeholder is a person or group that is impacted by the decisions and actions of another group. For example students are stakeholders in the actions of a school.

Possible Stakeholders for LLM Assisted Grading:

  • Students
  • Professors
  • Admin
  • IT/security
  • LLM developers

Students

Students are one of the primary stakeholders in this case, and their main interest from this system is fast feedback and transparency. The students want their assignments back quickly and with the understanding of what they got right or wrong and why they got the grade they did. Their main concern is the potential unethical bias of the LLMs and how that might affect their grades. This system would impact the students by significantly influencing the grades they receive.

Professors

Professors are the other primary stakeholders in this case, and their main interests are getting consistent, accurate grades to the students quickly. Their main concern is the potential inaccuracy of the LLMs, because if they have to go back and make all sorts of corrections and spend the time grading the assignments by hand, then the LLMs are for nought. They also are the ones responsible for making the rubrics and teaching the material, which is not all that different with the assistance of LLMs.

Admin

The school administration is one of the secondary stakeholders in this case, and their main interests are maintaining a good reputation and managing the legality of the situation. They don't want to get a bad reputation from the LLMs not working, or otherwise causing issues, and they don't want legal issues stemming from the use of LLM assisted grading. They don't necessarily care about the individual student. As for how they could be impacted, if there are issues, the administration takes the blow, they are seen as responsible for the whole program and have to suffer the consequences if things go wrong.

IT/Security

IT/security are one of the secondary stakeholders in this case, and their main interests are maintaining data privacy for the students and the school. They want to make sure that the student’s work is not “escaping” the grading process as well as making sure those grades stay private. As for how they could be impacted, any data leaks are their responsibility and they will suffer the consequences.

LLM developers

The LLM developers are one of the secondary stakeholders in this case, and their main interests are making sure that their LLM is providing accurate information consistently. They also want to minimize the bias their LLM has. Lastly they probably want to continue to build their LLM with the new data.

A Deeper Dive Into the Process of LLM Assisted Grading

The Process of LLM Assited Grading

LLM-assisted grading follows a human-in-the-loop approach when it comes to grading assignments. The process goes like this: An LLM is given a rubric made by the professor, and then it is given the student responses or work to grade based on that rubric. The LLM will then provide a provisional grade and a certainty score, so that the result can be reviewed by the professor or graders. Typically the LLM(s) are given the same assignment multiple times to establish an average grade to be more consistent and accurate. Additionally, by prompting the LLM(s) multiple times, any student response that falls outside of the LLM’s training will receive a range of scores as the AI hallucinates, and tries to understand the response (AI hallucination is complicated, if you would like to know how that works visit: What are AI hallucinations?). Furthermore, one could vary the way the LLM(s) are prompted in order to gain a more consistent/accurate average score and certainty.
Since LLMs are trained by humans there is some potential for the trainer's implicit bias to leak into the LLM. Ideally that is not the case, and the LLMs are robotically neutral, but it would be irresbonsible to not even consider the possiblility. Ultimately professors have the responsiblity to use the LLMs with little to no known biases. In a case where there is bias or a student feels like their work is being graded unfairly, they should have the right to appeal their grade, and have the professor grade it directly.

Evaluated Options

Option 1: Embrace LLM-assisted grading completely

  • Pros: Massive time save for professors and graders, faster feedback for students, removes human bias/error
  • Cons: introduces AI bias/errors
  • Stakeholder impact: Teachers get to save time which they can allocate to more useful endeavors, students might feel like data points, Institution gets to save cost.
  • The Deontology Lens: This option risks violating the right to human review if there is no transparent appeal process.
  • The Virtue Ethics Lens: By implementing this option you may eliminate the active learning that happens in the grader process, however the person recieving the grader may get a better of worse grade depending on implementation
  • The Utilitarian Lens: This option is highly efficient. So assuming it works well it does a lot of good, providing quick feedback and allowing the relocation of resources. If there are frequent mistakes, there is more work created which outways the good.
  • The Communitarian Lens: This serves the community by overall saving time for professors to focus on their teaching, in this sense the community as a whole would benefit even if the LLM Assisted grader works unevenly.

Option 2: Use LLM-assisted graders in a limited capacity

  • Pros: Balances efficiency with oversight; allows for "human-in-the-loop" verification; uses the tool where it’s strongest (pattern recognition).
  • Cons: Can create a "two-tier" system where some work is valued more than others; requires significant effort to define strict boundaries for use.
  • Stakeholder impact: Students receive quick feedback on basics and deep feedback on core concepts; Faculty remain the ultimate authority.
  • The Deontology Lens: This is the "fairest" option. It gives students what is their right: efficiency for routine tasks and human mentorship for complex growth.
  • The Virtue Ethics Lens: By maintaining human oversight on important work, this option acknowledges the emotional weight of grading and the relationship between teacher and pupil.
  • The Utilitarian Lens: By having a nuanced approach you may be missing the ability to maximize all possible good even if the decision would help.
  • The Communitarian Lens: This serves the community best by modernizing the institution without sacrificing the traditional values of academic mentorship.

Option 3: Reject LLM-assisted grading

  • Pros: Total transparency; avoids bias inherent in training data; preserves the traditional sanctity of the teacher-student bond.
  • Cons: Slower feedback loops; high burnout rates for educators; failure to prepare students for a world where AI interaction is the norm.
  • Stakeholder Impact: Faculty are overworked; Students wait weeks for grades; The institution may be seen as "outdated."
  • The Deontology Lens: Fully respects the student's right to human labor and attention.
  • The Utilitarian Lens: Low efficiency. The "harm" here is the delay in feedback, which is crucial for the learning process. Slow feedback can be a significant pedagogical failure.
  • The Virtue Ethics Lens: Acts as a "traditionalist" or "purist." It shows a commitment to the craft of teaching but perhaps a lack of adaptability to the needs of a changing society.
  • The Communitarianism Lens: When considering how rejecting LLM-assisted graders would affect the community it has the potential to bring further equality to grading systems.
